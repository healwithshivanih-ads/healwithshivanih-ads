# VitaOne Coaching Practice Guide

## Core Client-Facing Concepts

### The Functional Health Approach
Functional medicine focuses on identifying and addressing the underlying causes of disease rather than just treating symptoms. This approach views the body as an interconnected system where dysfunction in one area impacts overall health.

**Key principles for client communication:**
- Health exists on a spectrum, not as a binary of sick/well
- Root cause investigation is the foundation of healing
- Lifestyle modifications are powerful medicine
- The body has remarkable capacity for self-healing when given proper support
- Personalization is essential—there are no one-size-fits-all solutions

### Systems Integration
Help clients understand that their body functions as an integrated system:
- **Gut health** influences immune function, mood, energy, and hormone metabolism
- **Stress and sleep** affect every physiological system
- **Movement and exercise** impact metabolic health, hormone balance, and immune function
- **Nutrition** provides both fuel and information to every cell
- **Social connection and meaning** have measurable physiological effects

### The Concept of Functional Imbalance
Rather than waiting for disease diagnosis, functional medicine identifies patterns of dysfunction:
- Elevated fasting glucose before diabetes diagnosis
- Thyroid antibodies before TSH becomes abnormal
- Inflammatory markers before disease manifestation
- Hormonal imbalances affecting symptoms

This allows intervention at earlier, more correctable stages.

## Client Lifestyle Interventions

### Nutritional Approaches

**Anti-inflammatory foundation:**
The baseline for most functional health coaching is an anti-inflammatory eating pattern that emphasizes:
- Whole foods over processed
- Adequate protein at each meal
- Healthy fats (omega-3s, monounsaturated fats)
- Abundant vegetables and fruits
- Limited refined carbohydrates and added sugars
- Elimination of seed oils and trans fats

**Elimination diets:**
When specific food sensitivities are suspected, structured elimination protocols help identify triggers:
- Eliminate suspected foods for 3-4 weeks minimum
- Reintroduce one food at a time
- Monitor symptoms carefully
- Common elimination targets: gluten, dairy, eggs, soy, corn, nuts

**Functional nutrition patterns:**
- **Mediterranean-style eating** for cardiovascular and metabolic health
- **Lower carbohydrate approaches** for insulin resistance and weight management
- **Intermittent fasting** for metabolic flexibility and cellular autophagy
- **Cyclical patterns** that match activity level and circadian rhythm

### Sleep Optimization

Sleep is fundamental to healing and health. Coach clients on:

**Sleep hygiene fundamentals:**
- Consistent sleep schedule (within 1 hour)
- Dark, cool bedroom (60-67°F optimal)
- No screens 1-2 hours before bed
- Morning light exposure (within 30-60 minutes of waking)
- Evening wind-down routine
- Limited caffeine after 2 PM
- No alcohol close to bedtime (disrupts sleep architecture)

**Sleep-supporting supplements:**
- Magnesium glycinate (200-400mg before bed)
- L-theanine (100-200mg)
- Melatonin (0.5-3mg, lower doses often most effective)
- Glycine (3-5g)
- Consider timing: some clients respond better to supplements earlier in evening

**When to investigate deeper:**
- Persistent insomnia despite good hygiene
- Frequent nighttime waking
- Unrefreshed sleep despite adequate hours
- Sleep apnea indicators (snoring, witnessed pauses, daytime somnolence)

### Movement and Exercise

Physical activity supports health through multiple pathways:

**Exercise types and their functional benefits:**
- **Resistance training:** Builds muscle mass, improves metabolic health, supports bone density, improves insulin sensitivity
- **Aerobic exercise:** Supports cardiovascular health, improves mood, enhances mitochondrial function
- **Walking:** Low-impact, accessible, supports metabolic health and mental health
- **Flexibility and mobility work:** Supports nervous system regulation, reduces pain patterns
- **High-intensity interval training (HIIT):** Effective for metabolic improvement with time efficiency

**Practical coaching guidance:**
- Match exercise intensity to stress levels (avoid excessive high-intensity exercise during high stress)
- Progressive overload principles for continual adaptation
- Recovery is where adaptation happens
- Sleep and nutrition must support exercise level
- Individual variation is significant—some thrive with more intensity, others with moderate steady-state

### Stress Management and Nervous System Regulation

Chronic stress dysregulates nearly every physiological system. Coach clients on:

**Nervous system awareness:**
- Recognize signs of sympathetic dominance (anxiety, racing thoughts, difficulty sleeping)
- Recognize signs of parasympathetic dominance (fatigue, difficulty motivating, brain fog)
- Understand that stress physiology is real and measurable
- Validate that their experience of stress has biological consequences

**Practical stress management tools:**
- **Breathing practices:** 4-7-8 breathing, box breathing, coherent breathing (5-6 breaths per minute)
- **Meditation and mindfulness:** Even 5-10 minutes daily shows benefits
- **Progressive muscle relaxation:** Systematic tension and release
- **Time in nature:** Measurable nervous system benefits
- **Creative expression:** Art, music, writing as stress processing
- **Social connection:** Fundamental to stress resilience
- **Meaning and purpose:** Strong predictor of health outcomes

**Adaptogenic herbs:**
- Rhodiola, ashwagandha, eleuthero, holy basil
- Best used short-term (3-4 months) as support while building lasting practices
- Individual response varies significantly

## Clinical Education for Clients

### Inflammation Fundamentals

Help clients understand that inflammation exists on a spectrum:

**Acute inflammation (helpful):**
- Protective immune response to injury or infection
- Resolves when the threat is removed
- Necessary for healing

**Chronic inflammation (problematic):**
- Low-grade, persistent activation of immune system
- May not have obvious symptoms
- Underlying driver of most chronic diseases
- Measurable through inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR, cytokines)

**Client-friendly explanation:**
"Imagine your immune system has a dimmer switch that should turn off when the threat passes. In chronic inflammation, that switch gets stuck in the 'on' position. Your body stays in a defensive state even when there's no active threat. This constant defensive activation damages tissues and speeds aging."

**Drivers of chronic inflammation:**
- Chronic stress and poor sleep
- Processed foods and seed oils
- Intestinal permeability
- Dysbiosis (imbalanced gut bacteria)
- Environmental toxins
- Lack of movement
- Chronic infections
- Autoimmune activation

### Insulin Resistance

One of the most prevalent and impactful functional imbalances in modern populations.

**Simple explanation for clients:**
"Insulin is like a key that unlocks your cells to let glucose in. When you have insulin resistance, your cells don't respond well to the key. Your pancreas has to make more and more insulin to get glucose into cells. This high circulating insulin causes weight gain, inflammation, and increased disease risk."

**Clinical markers:**
- Fasting glucose >100 mg/dL
- Fasting insulin >12 mIU/L
- HOMA-IR >2.5
- Triglycerides/HDL ratio >2
- A1C >5.7%

**Progression if untreated:**
- Metabolic syndrome
- Type 2 diabetes
- Cardiovascular disease
- Cognitive decline
- Increased cancer risk

**Intervention priorities:**
1. Reduce refined carbohydrate intake
2. Increase physical activity (especially resistance training)
3. Support sleep and stress management
4. Optimize gut health
5. Consider medications if lifestyle modifications insufficient

### Thyroid Function Beyond TSH

Most thyroid screening only checks TSH, missing important dysfunction patterns.

**Complete thyroid assessment includes:**
- TSH
- Free T4
- Free T3
- Thyroid peroxidase (TPO) antibodies
- Thyroglobulin antibodies
- Sometimes: reverse T3, thyroid hormone binding proteins

**Functional patterns:**
- **Subclinical hypothyroidism:** TSH elevated, free hormones still in range (symptomatic treatment often helpful)
- **Central hypothyroidism:** Low TSH with low free T4 (pituitary/hypothalamic issue)
- **Hashimoto's thyroiditis:** Antibodies present (autoimmune thyroid disease)
- **Thyroid hormone conversion issues:** Normal T4, low T3 (suggests stress, liver dysfunction, or inadequate selenium/zinc)

**Non-pharmaceutical support for thyroid:**
- **Selenium:** Critical for thyroid peroxidase function, 200 mcg daily
- **Zinc:** Supports thyroid hormone conversion and immune regulation
- **Iron:** Essential for thyroid peroxidase; iron deficiency impairs thyroid function
- **Iodine:** Essential for hormone synthesis; balance critical (too much can trigger autoimmunity)
- **Stress reduction:** Stress impairs thyroid function
- **Gut health:** Needed for hormone absorption and conversion

## Referral Protocols

### When to Refer to MDs/Specialists

**Always refer to MD:**
- Chest pain or shortness of breath
- Symptoms of stroke
- Severe abdominal pain
- Signs of severe infection
- Mental health crisis
- New neurological symptoms
- Suspected malignancy
- Significant laboratory abnormalities

**Consider specialist referral:**
- Persistent symptoms despite intervention
- Complex medical history
- Multiple medication interactions
- Need for diagnostic imaging
- Surgical considerations

### Collaborative Care Approach

**Positioning the coaching relationship:**
- Complement, not replace, medical care
- Share relevant findings with client's MD
- Request permission to communicate with healthcare providers
- Understand scope of practice boundaries
- Build referral relationships with progressive-minded MDs

**Documentation for referral:**
- Summarize assessment findings
- Note interventions attempted and client response
- Provide context on client's health goals
- Ask specific questions if referral is appropriate

## Seven Clinical Focus Areas

### 1. Thyroid Health

**Assessment priorities:**
- Complete thyroid panel (TSH, free T4, free T3, TPO, thyroglobulin antibodies)
- Symptoms: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, hair loss, constipation, brain fog
- Functional T3 decline before TSH elevation
- Reverse T3 elevation (indicates stress, illness, or inadequate nutrition)

**Coaching interventions:**
- Identify and address nutrient deficiencies (selenium, zinc, iron, iodine)
- Reduce stress through all available means
- Optimize sleep and circadian rhythm
- Support gut health (thyroid hormone reabsorption in intestines)
- Consider elimination of goitrogens if Hashimoto's present (though some sources debate significance)
- Support medication absorption if on thyroid replacement (take on empty stomach, away from supplements)

**Red flags requiring MD input:**
- TSH >10 or <0.1
- Rapidly rising antibodies
- Symptoms severe despite medication
- New onset symptoms despite stable medication

### 2. Insulin Resistance

**Assessment priorities:**
- Fasting glucose and insulin
- HOMA-IR calculation
- Triglycerides and HDL cholesterol
- A1C for long-term glucose patterns
- Waist circumference (visceral fat indicator)
- Blood pressure and metabolic syndrome criteria

**Coaching interventions:**
- Dietary: Lower refined carbohydrates, increase soluble fiber, adequate protein
- Movement: Both aerobic and resistance training highly effective
- Sleep: Sleep deprivation significantly impairs insulin sensitivity
- Stress: Chronic stress drives insulin resistance
- Specific supplements: Inositol (myo-inositol and d-chiro-inositol), chromium, berberine
- Timing: Consider meal timing and intermittent fasting approaches

**Monitoring progress:**
- Recheck labs every 3-6 months
- Track clinical markers: weight, energy, sleep quality, cravings
- Celebrate non-scale victories: improved labs, better energy, improved mood

### 3. PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome)

**Clinical presentation:**
- Irregular or absent periods
- Elevated androgens (acne, hirsutism, male-pattern hair loss)
- Polycystic appearance on ultrasound (not always present)
- Often accompanied by insulin resistance and weight management difficulty

**Assessment priorities:**
- Hormone panel: testosterone, DHEA-S, LH, FSH, prolactin
- Metabolic markers: fasting glucose, insulin, HOMA-IR
- Thyroid function (common co-occurrence)
- Inflammatory markers: CRP
- Ultrasound findings if available

**Coaching interventions:**
- **Address insulin resistance:** This is the primary modifiable factor
- **Anti-inflammatory diet:** Particularly important given PCOS inflammatory nature
- **Inositol supplementation:** Myo-inositol and d-chiro-inositol ratio critical (often 40:1)
- **Adequate protein:** Supports satiety and metabolic health
- **Regular movement:** Improves insulin sensitivity and hormonal balance
- **Stress management:** Elevates cortisol which exacerbates PCOS
- **Sleep optimization:** Sleep deprivation worsens insulin resistance
- **Consider spearmint tea:** Some evidence for anti-androgenic effects

**Fertility considerations:**
- Improving insulin sensitivity often improves fertility
- Weight loss (even 5-10%) can restore ovulation
- Supplement with folate due to increased miscarriage risk

### 4. Menopause and Hormonal Transitions

**Transitional phases:**
- Perimenopause (10-15 years of hormonal fluctuation)
- Menopause (last menstrual period)
- Postmenopause (after final period)

**Key physiological changes:**
- Declining estrogen and progesterone
- Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) elevation
- Shifts in bone density, cardiovascular risk, body composition
- Changes in mood, sleep, and vasomotor symptoms

**Assessment priorities:**
- Symptom pattern (hot flashes, night sweats, mood changes, sleep disruption, vaginal dryness)
- Current hormone levels if helpful (though patterns often more important than numbers)
- Cardiovascular risk assessment
- Bone density screening
- Mental health screening

**Coaching interventions:**
- **Phytoestrogen foods:** Flaxseeds, legumes, soy (if tolerated)
- **Adequate protein:** Supports muscle maintenance and bone health
- **Strength training:** Maintains bone density and muscle mass
- **Cardiovascular exercise:** Supports metabolic and cardiovascular health
- **Sleep optimization:** Often significantly disrupted; prioritize heavily
- **Stress management:** Hormonal fluctuations amplify stress response
- **Herbal support:** Black cohosh, red clover, sage for vasomotor symptoms
- **Vaginal health:** Adequate moisture, pelvic floor health
- **Sexual function support:** Libido changes are normal; communication important

**Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) conversations:**
- Provide evidence-based information
- Acknowledge individual variation in symptom severity
- Understand that some clients will choose HRT and that's valid
- Support is still valuable alongside HRT

### 5. Cholesterol and Cardiovascular Health

**Paradigm shift from total cholesterol:**
- Total cholesterol number less meaningful than lipid particle patterns
- LDL particle number more predictive than LDL cholesterol
- HDL and triglycerides crucial markers
- Inflammatory markers (CRP, Lp(a)) important for risk assessment

**Assessment priorities:**
- Lipid panel: total, LDL, HDL, triglycerides
- Advanced lipid testing: LDL particle number, small dense LDL, Lp(a)
- Inflammatory markers: high-sensitivity CRP, homocysteine
- Blood pressure
- Blood glucose and insulin
- Family history

**Coaching interventions:**
- **Dietary fat quality:** Emphasis on omega-3s and monounsaturated fats
- **Soluble fiber:** Oats, beans, apples help lower cholesterol
- **Plant sterols:** Nuts, seeds, whole grains
- **Adequate exercise:** Improves all lipid markers
- **Stress reduction:** Stress dysregulates lipids
- **Sleep:** Sleep deprivation worsens lipid patterns
- **Weight management:** If overweight; even 5-10% loss improves lipids
- **Supplement support:** Fish oil (if tolerated), CoQ10 if on statins, niacin

**Red flags requiring MD assessment:**
- Severely elevated cholesterol (>300)
- Elevated Lp(a) (genetic factor)
- Very low HDL (<30)
- Very high triglycerides (>400)
- Concurrent elevated inflammatory markers

### 6. Autoimmune Conditions

**Common presentations:**
- Celiac disease (gluten-triggered intestinal autoimmunity)
- Hashimoto's thyroiditis (thyroid antibodies)
- Rheumatoid arthritis (joint-focused autoimmunity)
- Lupus (systemic autoimmunity)
- Type 1 diabetes (pancreatic beta-cell autoimmunity)
- Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis)

**Underlying mechanisms (simplified):**
- Genetic predisposition
- Environmental trigger (often infection or dietary antigen)
- Intestinal permeability ("leaky gut")
- Dysbiosis (imbalanced microbiome)
- Chronic stress and immune dysregulation

**Assessment priorities:**
- Specific antibody testing (tissue-specific and general autoimmune markers)
- Inflammatory markers: ESR, CRP
- Intestinal permeability assessment: zonulin, lipopolysaccharide (LPS)
- Microbiome assessment if tools available
- Food sensitivity testing if appropriate
- Stress and sleep patterns

**Coaching interventions:**
- **Remove trigger if identified:** Most important intervention (e.g., gluten in celiac)
- **Heal intestinal lining:** Bone broth, L-glutamine, zinc carnosine
- **Anti-inflammatory diet:** Eliminate processed foods, seed oils; emphasize vegetables
- **Restore microbiome balance:** Fiber, fermented foods, strategic probiotics
- **Immune regulation:** Adequate vitamin D, omega-3s, stress reduction
- **Sleep and stress:** These directly regulate immune function
- **Movement:** Gentle to moderate exercise supports immune balance
- **Specific supplements:** Curcumin, omega-3s, vitamin D, sometimes specific probiotics

**When to refer:**
- Newly diagnosed autoimmune condition (need MD monitoring)
- Flare activity during coaching
- Considering immunosuppressive medications
- Multiple autoimmune conditions

### 7. Chronic Inflammation

**Manifestations:**
- Low-grade fever or feeling unwell
- Fatigue and low energy
- Brain fog and difficulty concentrating
- Joint or muscle pain
- Frequent infections or slow healing
- Digestive symptoms
- Anxiety or mood changes
- Weight management difficulty

**Assessment priorities:**
- Inflammatory markers: CRP (high-sensitivity), ESR, cytokines if available
- Identify sources: infections, dysbiosis, intestinal permeability, food sensitivities, stress, sleep
- Screen for underlying autoimmune conditions
- Assess environmental toxin exposure
- Evaluate stress levels and sleep quality

**Coaching interventions:**
- **Identify and remove sources:** Most important step
  - Food sensitivities (elimination diet)
  - Chronic infections (may need MD evaluation)
  - Dysbiosis (gut health optimization)
  - Environmental toxins (air quality, water, personal care products)
  - Psychological stress (stress management practices)
  - Sleep deprivation (sleep optimization)

- **Anti-inflammatory foods:** Vegetables, omega-3 rich foods, herbs and spices (turmeric, ginger)
- **Eliminate pro-inflammatory foods:** Refined carbs, seed oils, processed foods
- **Gut healing:** Remove triggers, add healing foods, consider antimicrobial or antifungal support if dysbiosis
- **Supplement support:**
  - Omega-3 fatty acids
  - Curcumin (from turmeric)
  - Resveratrol (from grapes, berries)
  - Alpha-lipoic acid
  - N-acetylcysteine (NAC)
  - Vitamin D

- **Movement:** Gentle to moderate exercise is anti-inflammatory
- **Stress management:** Chronic stress perpetuates inflammation
- **Sleep:** Sleep deprivation perpetuates inflammatory cascade
- **Social connection:** Isolation perpetuates inflammation; connection is healing

**Monitoring:**
- Repeat inflammatory markers every 3-6 months
- Track symptom resolution
- Identify which interventions create most benefit for that individual

## Practice Excellence

### Session Structure

**Initial assessment (60-90 minutes):**
- Detailed health history and timeline
- Current symptoms and functional limitations
- Previous attempts at health improvement
- Nutrition and lifestyle assessment
- Sleep, stress, and movement patterns
- Health goals and values
- Lab review and recommendations for additional testing

**Follow-up sessions (45-60 minutes):**
- Review progress on previous action items
- Address obstacles and problem-solve
- Introduce next-level interventions
- Check in on lifestyle changes and adherence
- Adjust recommendations based on response
- Celebrate progress

**Session spacing:**
- Initial follow-up: 2-4 weeks
- Ongoing: typically every 4-6 weeks as client builds consistency
- May increase frequency during intense transitions or acute issues

### Building Client Agency

The most effective coaching creates clients who understand their own physiology and can make decisions independently.

**Principles:**
- Explain the "why" behind recommendations
- Help clients understand their own patterns
- Support experimentation and self-discovery
- Celebrate client insights
- Build capacity for problem-solving
- Gradually reduce dependence as competence increases
- Empower decision-making about trade-offs

### Documentation and Communication

**Records to maintain:**
- Initial assessment findings and goals
- Lab results and interpretation
- Recommendations and interventions
- Client response and adherence
- Progress toward goals
- Any referrals made

**Communication with other providers:**
- Request permission to share information
- Provide context on coaching approach and goals
- Share relevant findings
- Ask specific questions if referral indicated
- Request collaboration when appropriate

### Managing Complexity

**When client presentation is complex:**
- Start with foundational interventions (sleep, stress, movement, anti-inflammatory diet)
- Address highest-impact issues first
- Sequence introductions of new interventions
- Build on success
- Refer for additional expertise when needed
- Maintain clear communication about scope

**Setting boundaries:**
- Mental health concerns: may need mental health professional
- Severe medical complexity: may need MD specialist
- Medication adjustments: always coordinate with prescriber
- Nutritional deficiencies: may need supplementation specialist
- Specific conditions: may benefit from condition-specific expertise

## Special Populations

### Pregnancy and Postpartum

**Pregnancy considerations:**
- Nutritional needs increase significantly
- Movement modifications needed
- Sleep becomes more difficult
- Emotional support critical
- Avoid aggressive detoxification or elimination diets

**Postpartum focus:**
- Nutritional recovery and repletion
- Gentle movement reintroduction
- Sleep support (often limited by infant care)
- Emotional and mental health support
- Breastfeeding support if applicable
- Energy for daily functioning prioritized over appearance goals

### Aging Adults

**Age-related considerations:**
- Muscle mass preservation becomes critical
- Bone health paramount
- Chronic disease management likely
- Medication interactions important
- Sensory and functional changes affect nutrition
- Social connection increasingly important
- Legacy and meaning become more relevant

### Athletes and High-Performance Individuals

**Special considerations:**
- Higher nutrient needs (particularly iron, B vitamins, minerals)
- Recovery is training adaptation mechanism
- Sleep becomes a performance factor
- Chronic inflammation risk from overtraining
- Gut health impacts nutrient absorption and performance
- Psychological stress management as important as physical training

## Foundational Health Principles

### The Primacy of Basic Habits

The most effective interventions are often the most basic:
1. **Sleep:** 7-9 hours nightly is non-negotiable for health
2. **Movement:** 150+ minutes weekly of moderate activity
3. **Nutrition:** Whole foods, adequate protein, abundant vegetables
4. **Stress management:** Daily practice of nervous system regulation
5. **Social connection:** Regular meaningful interaction
6. **Purpose and meaning:** Activities that feel important

These five areas account for the majority of health variation. Supplements and specific interventions amplify but cannot replace these foundations.

### Individual Variation

There is no single optimal diet, exercise, or lifestyle approach. Variation exists in:
- Nutrient needs (some clients need more iron, others more copper)
- Food tolerances (some thrive on higher carbohydrates, others on lower)
- Exercise preferences (some prefer intensity, others prefer steady-state)
- Stress management tools (some need movement, others need stillness)
- Sleep needs (average is 7-9, but range exists)

Effective coaching helps clients discover their individual optimal patterns.

### The Gut-Everything Connection

The gastrointestinal tract influences nearly all aspects of health:
- **Nutrient absorption:** Cannot benefit from nutrition if not absorbed
- **Immune function:** 70-80% of immune system lives in gut
- **Hormone metabolism:** Gut bacteria influence estrogen, serotonin, other hormones
- **Brain function:** Gut-brain axis influences mood, cognition, anxiety
- **Inflammation:** Dysbiosis and intestinal permeability drive systemic inflammation
- **Energy:** Gut dysbiosis contributes to fatigue

Gut optimization is often foundational to addressing other health issues.

### The Stress-Immunity Connection

Chronic stress dysregulates immune function through multiple pathways:
- Elevated cortisol suppresses immune function and increases inflammation
- Depletes neurotransmitters needed for emotional resilience
- Impairs digestion and nutrient absorption
- Disrupts sleep architecture
- Increases susceptibility to infection
- Accelerates aging

Stress management is not optional—it's foundational medicine.

### Timeline Expectations

Healing takes time. Set realistic expectations:
- **2-4 weeks:** Sleep and energy improvements often noted
- **4-8 weeks:** Mood, digestion, and cravings often shift
- **8-12 weeks:** Weight changes, lab improvements, cognitive changes emerging
- **3-6 months:** Significant shifts in how client feels and lab markers
- **6-12 months:** Deep healing and pattern change
- **12+ months:** Sustained improvement and lifestyle integration

Communicate this timeline to manage expectations and maintain motivation through the process.

## Ethical Practice

### Informed Consent

Clients should understand:
- The coaching relationship is not a substitute for medical care
- Your qualifications and scope of practice
- What they can expect from coaching
- Any potential risks (e.g., dietary changes affecting medications)
- The importance of working with their healthcare providers
- Their right to decline recommendations

### Scope of Practice

Know your boundaries and stay within them:
- Do not diagnose
- Do not prescribe medications
- Do not interpret diagnostic testing beyond your training
- Do not provide mental health treatment
- Do not make guarantees about outcomes
- Refer when client needs exceed your expertise

### Confidentiality

Maintain client privacy:
- Secure records
- Confidential communication
- No sharing without explicit permission
- Understand legal requirements in your jurisdiction

### Cultural Competence

Recognize and respect:
- Dietary and cultural food practices
- Different health beliefs
- Religious and spiritual practices
- Socioeconomic factors affecting health access
- Racial and ethnic health disparities
- Individual values and priorities

### Working with Vulnerability

Many clients seeking health coaching are in vulnerable positions:
- Dealing with chronic illness or loss of health
- Emotional patterns around food and body
- Past trauma affecting health behaviors
- Significant health anxiety
- Financial stress affecting health access

Approach with:
- Compassion and non-judgment
- Recognition of trauma's impact
- Attention to power dynamics
- Appropriate boundaries
- Referral to mental health support when needed

This guide provides the foundation for evidence-informed, client-centered functional health coaching. Effective coaching combines this knowledge with authentic relationship, curiosity about individual patterns, and genuine commitment to client wellbeing.
