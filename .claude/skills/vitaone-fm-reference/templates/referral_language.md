# Referral Language Templates

Ways to phrase "you need a clinician for this" that maintain trust and don't feel dismissive. Referring well is part of good coaching — it builds credibility and protects both client and coach.

---

## The core principle

Referrals aren't a failure. They're a signal that you take the client's whole picture seriously. Frame them as *expanding* the team, not *ending* the conversation.

---

## General templates

### When labs are needed

> "Some of what you're describing needs labs to pin down properly — and that's outside what I can order or interpret. Let's build a list of tests to ask your clinician about, and I'll help you come to that appointment prepared with the right questions."

### When the client asks about specific supplements

> "I can't give you a specific supplement protocol — that's prescribing, which a coach can't do. What I can do is tell you the lifestyle and food foundations that research supports for what you're dealing with, and help you identify the right type of clinician to talk to about supplements. That's actually the more durable starting point anyway."

### When the client wants you to interpret their labs

> "I can help you understand what each test measures and what the general principles are. I can't tell you what your specific values mean for your body — that needs a clinician who's seen your full picture. What I can help with is framing the questions to ask so you get more from your next appointment."

### When a client has a diagnosed condition

> "Great — your clinician is the one steering that. My job is to amplify the lifestyle foundations alongside what they've prescribed, and to help you implement the things that often get skipped over in standard appointments: sleep, stress, food patterns, movement. Let's make sure those are solid."

## Specific referrals

### Referring to an FM physician (MD / BAMS / ND)

> "What you're describing has a lot of moving parts — thyroid, hormones, gut, stress all talking to each other. A Functional Medicine-trained physician is someone who can order the full panel of tests and put the whole picture together. Want help finding one in your area?"

**Red flag phrasing:** "This is more than lifestyle work can solve on its own. You deserve the full workup."

### Referring to a registered dietitian (RD) or clinical nutritionist

> "For [pregnancy / eating disorder history / diagnosed condition requiring medical nutrition therapy], I'd want you working with a registered dietitian who can make specific dietary prescriptions — that's a different scope than mine. I can keep working with you on the behavioral and habit side once you have that in place."

### Referring to mental health

**For non-urgent:**
> "A lot of what's coming up — [symptom pattern] — sits in territory where a trauma-informed therapist or psychologist would add more than I can. It's not that your body stuff isn't real; it's that the nervous system piece is often upstream, and a specialist there can help us move faster."

**For urgent:**
> "What you're describing is bigger than our work together can hold safely. I want to make sure you have the right support right now — [specific crisis resource or trusted clinician]. Can we make that call together now, or can I send you resources after this?"

### Referring to a specialist

**Cardiology:**
> "With your family history [or lab pattern], a cardiologist should be part of the team. Modern preventive cardiology does a lot more than 'don't eat eggs' — they can look at advanced markers like ApoB and Lp(a) that the standard lipid panel misses."

**Endocrinology / thyroid specialist:**
> "Thyroid is specialized enough that an endocrinologist or Functional Medicine-trained thyroid specialist is worth the investment. Ask for: full panel including Free T3, Free T4, TPO antibodies, TgAb — not just TSH."

**Gynecology / menopause:**
> "Menopause care has evolved a lot, and there are now clinicians specifically trained in modern menopause care — [NAMS certified in the US, FMCN-trained FM physicians in India]. For the HRT conversation specifically, that's who you want."

**Gastroenterology:**
> "Persistent gut symptoms deserve a workup — some things (like SIBO, H. pylori, IBD, celiac) need specific testing that only a GI can run. Coaching-level gut work is powerful, but it works best alongside a proper diagnostic workup."

**Dermatology:**
> "Acne at [age] with [pattern] is worth a dermatology consult alongside our hormonal/dietary work. They can rule out anything structural and, if appropriate, coordinate with your FM clinician on the systemic piece."

**Sleep medicine:**
> "Sleep apnea isn't just snoring — it affects blood sugar, cardiovascular risk, cognition. A sleep study ($ up front, but clear answer) is worth prioritizing if any of the markers fit."

**Dental (for oral-systemic link):**
> "This sounds weird but follow me: gum inflammation drives systemic CRP and cardiovascular risk. If you haven't had a dental cleaning in a while, that's a legit health intervention, not just cosmetic."

## When the client pushes back

### "Can't you just tell me what to take?"

> "I know it would be faster if I just said 'take X.' But here's why I won't: (1) coaching scope — I can't prescribe, full stop. (2) Bio-individuality is real — what works for one person backfires in another based on their labs and context. (3) Supplements can interact with medications in ways only your clinician has the full picture for. What I can do — and where coaching outperforms random supplement advice — is the behavioral and lifestyle layer that actually determines whether anything else works."

### "My friend takes [supplement] for [condition] — can I?"

> "Maybe — but she's her and you're you, and neither of us has enough of the picture to know. What I can tell you is what the research says about [supplement] as education, and what questions to bring to a clinician who can actually assess whether it's right for your labs."

### "Can you just look at my labs?"

> "I can help you understand what each test measures and what the general research says about those markers. I can't interpret your specific values — that crosses into diagnosis. But I'll help you prepare to get more out of your clinician conversation: what to ask, what to follow up on, what to track between visits."

### "But [influencer / FM doc on Instagram] said..."

> "Yeah, a lot of FM content online is compelling but the evidence behind specific claims varies a lot. I can tell you what the research says about [specific claim] — sometimes it's better than mainstream medicine presents, sometimes it's thinner than FM content suggests. That's one of my jobs here — to help you separate what's well-supported from what's more speculative."

## Crisis / urgent referrals (non-negotiable)

If any of these come up, drop everything else and direct the client to appropriate care. Don't hedge.

- **Suicidal ideation or active self-harm plans:** "What you're telling me is something that needs immediate support. Are you safe right now?" → connect to crisis resources.
- **Chest pain, severe shortness of breath, suspected stroke (FAST symptoms):** "Stop our session. Call emergency services or go to an ER right now."
- **Severe abdominal pain, blood in vomit or stool, high fever with stiff neck:** "This is ER territory, not our session territory."
- **New neurological symptoms — sudden weakness, speech changes, vision loss, severe headache:** "Urgent medical care."
- **Suspected anaphylaxis or severe allergic reaction:** "Epi-pen if you have one; emergency services either way."
- **Post-menopausal vaginal bleeding:** "This always gets urgent gynecology evaluation. Same week if possible."

## Building your referral network (India-specific considerations)

For Shivani: over time, curate a list of clinicians you trust in these categories. Having the referral ready makes the handoff smooth and keeps the client in good hands.

- Functional Medicine physicians (FMCN/IFMCP-certified; BAMS with integrative training)
- Registered dietitians with FM background
- Endocrinologists familiar with FM principles
- Menopause-literate gynecologists
- Trauma-informed therapists (EMDR, Somatic Experiencing, IFS trained)
- Sleep medicine specialists
- Functional dentists (biological dentistry, aware of oral-systemic link)

When you don't have a specific referral, you can still point the client toward how to find one: "Look for [certification type]" or "Ask about their approach to [X]."

---

## Notes

- These are *starting* language. Adapt to your voice and the specific client.
- Never refer *and abandon*. Stay connected to the client's journey even when a specialist is driving the clinical piece.
- Document your referral reasoning in your session notes — protects you and improves your future practice.
